Anaconda Enterprise V5

This product had a lot of complexity and ambiguity; the best strategy was to move quickly and embrace change. It was important to know when to use the right tool, deliver the right fidelity and level of documentation.

Context

In the last quarter of 2016, we began building our enterprise-ready data science platform. The core team consisted of the product manager, one developer and me. Some of the basic architecture had been built. Together, we scoped out the features and wrote user stories. I also drafted the first official design process for the product team.

Constraints

I was a “UX team of one” so, I had limited time & resources. We experienced frequent changes in direction, deadlines and teams. Based on certain clients, the UI had strict accessibility guidelines. Due to the confidentiality of our clients and data, we relied on input from internal data scientists and client-facing sales engineers. We also experienced a set of development challenges due to the underlying architecture of the platform.

Process

I researched, conducted interviews and competitive analysis to gather as much insight about the product and data science workflows. I explored vastly differently directions collaboratively, by conducting design studios with various colleagues. I received feedback on wireframes from the team and created clickable prototypes to run usability tests with more people. Based on the feedback, I explored some architecture and layout iterations.

Around this time, the product direction and deadline shifted, and our team grew. Since we had more developers building components, we needed a set of UI standards. We implemented the existing marketing brand guide, but it lacked many product components and didn’t meet our accessibility requirements. So, I designed a formal product UI kit. Shortly after implementation, the innovator program launched (a month earlier than we anticipated).

At this stage, the company restructured, which affected the product direction once again. Our team changed again and also, a lead designer joined the product team! This new addition brought upon some major changes to the product UI and design process. With the addition of developers to the project, designs were built faster, which required more documentation and communication. So, we created functional specifications to ensure that designs were implemented accurately. In the next couple of months, we launched GA.

[insert image of paper prototype - text: ] Launch is never the end. After the launch, we revisited old designs and explored new features and components. In order to get as much feedback as possible, we had lots of meetings, interviews and usability tests with various stakeholders. I organized much of this feedback into categories that and iterated designs to explore solutions. We also researched feedback loops which would allow us to interact directly with select participants. From here, we worked on a new visual direction and forward-looking features.

Lessons Learned (tl;dr)

Lean UX activities like design studios and a design book club helped create a favorable design culture. Less is more except for when it comes to communication. More documentation and presentations meant that teams had more visibility and accountability.
